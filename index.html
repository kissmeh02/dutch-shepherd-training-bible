Reward Markers & Praise

Teach these before any commands. The dog must understand what "yes" and "no" mean first.
Primary Yes Marker
Ja
YAH
Precise "yes!" marker — reward is coming now. Use the instant the correct behaviour happens.
General Praise
Goed zo
KHOOT ZO
"Good job / well done" — warm, ongoing praise. Not a precise marker, but builds confidence.
Enthusiastic Praise
Braaf
BRAHF
"Good dog / well behaved" — use for sustained good behaviour. Higher energy praise.
Release Word
Vrij
VREY
"Free / released" — every stay, wait, and position ends here. Never skip the release.
No / Correction
Nee
NAY
"No" — calm and firm, never shouted. Marks the wrong behaviour without emotion.
Strong Shame Marker
Foei
FOO-ee
"Shame / bad" — used sparingly for serious corrections only. Not for puppies learning.

Puppy Training Roadmap

What to teach, when to teach it.
8 – 10 Weeks
First Days Home — Orientation & Safety
Name recognition is job one. Establish crate comfort, sleep routine, and potty schedule. Introduce marker words Ja and Nee. Keep sessions under 3 minutes, 3–5x daily. Everything is a game.
NaamJaNeeDoe hetMand ✓ Crate = safe den, never punishment ✓ Socialization window open — expose to everything
10 – 12 Weeks
Focus & Impulse Control
Kijk (watch me) becomes the gateway to all training. Add Zit, Hier, Wacht, and Laat. Introduce bite inhibition with Zacht — this window closes by 16 weeks. Start scent games: hide treats, find toy.
KijkZitHierLaatZachtZoek ⚠ No forced positions — lure only at this age ✓ Bite inhibition must be taught NOW
12 – 16 Weeks
Core Obedience Block
Add Af, Blijf, Vrij, Stond. Begin loose leash walking toward Voet. Introduce Vriendelijk for controlled greetings. Puppy classes strongly recommended this phase. Duration stays up to 10 seconds.
AfBlijfVrijVoetVriendelijk ✓ End every session on success ✓ Socialization window closes ~16 weeks
4 – 6 Months
Drive Development & Sport Foundation
Build tug drive with Pak aan and Los. Introduce Apport with a structured retrieve chain. Begin formal heeling pattern. Add Tik (target) for body awareness. Start basic tracking with Spoor on short aged tracks.
Pak aanLosApportSpoorTik ⚠ No protection work before 12–18 months ✓ High drive = high reward; match their energy
6 – 12 Months
Adolescence — Hold the Line
Expect regression. The Dutch Shepherd adolescent will test everything. Maintain all foundational commands with consistency. Increase 3 D's on stays. Build distance heeling. Keep protection work to sport-club environment only. Channel energy into scent work and sport foundations.
⚠ Hardest phase — don't skip fundamentals ⚠ Intact males especially challenging 7–10 months ✓ 2+ hours exercise daily at this age ✓ Find an Ontario KNPV or IPO club
12 – 18 Months
Sport Transition & Advanced Work
Dog is mentally mature enough to begin structured protection sport under qualified guidance. Formalize IGP/KNPV tracking work. Off-leash obedience at distance. Begin Bewaken and Blaf/Stil on/off control. Trial prep if desired.
BewakenBlafStilAanSpoor artikel ✓ Protection sport MUST be club-based with decoy

Reading Your Dutch Shepherd

Understanding your dog is as important as commanding them.
Loose, wiggly body
Relaxed and happy. Ideal state for learning new behaviours.
Soft eyes, mouth slightly open
Calm and engaged. Good training state — focused without over-arousal.
High, fast tail wag
Aroused and excited. May be too stimulated for precision work. Use Rustig.
Play bow (front end down)
Wants to play. Healthy drive signal — use for reward in sport work.
Hard stare, still body, forward ears
Locked on to something. Pre-alert state. Intervene before escalation with Rustig.
Raised hackles (along back)
Aroused — can be excitement OR threat response. Read the full picture.
Lip lick, yawn, head turn
Stress or calming signal. Dog is uncomfortable. Reduce pressure — don't push.
Whale eye (whites showing)
Fearful or conflicted. Back off immediately. Do not correct — comfort and redirect.
Tail at body height, slow wag
Confident and content. Good baseline for this breed.
Intense eye contact at handler
Checking in — great sign of handler focus. Reward this heavily in early training.

The Handler's Rules

How you train matters as much as what you train.
🔒
One Command, One Cue
Never mix Dutch and English for the same behaviour. Pick one and stick to it forever. Consistency is the entire game.
⏱
Short Sessions, High Frequency
10–15 minutes maximum per session. 3–5 sessions daily beats one long grind. Always end on success.
🎯
Mark the Exact Moment
"Ja" must land within 1 second of the correct behaviour. Delayed markers teach the wrong thing. Timing is everything.
🚫
Never Use the Name as Punishment
The dog's name should always predict something good. Never shout it in frustration or pair it with corrections.
📈
Raise Criteria Slowly
Never increase duration, distance, AND distraction at the same time. Change one variable per session. 80% success rate before raising the bar.
💪
Match Drive to Task
Dutch Shepherds have enormous drive. Precision work needs a calm dog. Sport work needs a fired-up dog. Learn to bring them up and bring them down.
🧠
Mental Work Counts
A 15-minute scent session is worth an hour of physical exercise for this breed. Tired brain = calm dog.
🤝
Get a Club or Trainer
For protection and sport work especially, find an Ontario KNPV, IGP, or Dutch Shepherd club. A qualified decoy and trainer is non-negotiable for bite work.

Long-Haired DSD — Breed Notes

Things specific to the long-haired variety that affect training.
Coat management: Long coats mat around collar and harness wear points. Check weekly during training. Matted areas can cause discomfort that affects focus.
Softer temperament: Long-hairs tend to be slightly more sensitive than brindle short-coats. They respond better to praise-heavy training and are more affected by handler frustration.
Heat sensitivity: Long coats trap heat. Train in early morning in summer. Always have water. Watch for panting during outdoor sessions above 22°C.
Hip dysplasia: Common in the breed. Avoid high-impact repetitive work (lots of jumping) before 18 months. OFA/PennHIP screen at 2 years.
Eye conditions: Hereditary cataracts reported. Annual eye exams recommended. Watch for squinting during bright outdoor training.
High exercise need: 2+ hours daily minimum at maturity. Inadequate exercise is the #1 cause of problem behaviours in this breed. Walk, run, work.
Intelligence & boredom: Bored Dutch Shepherds become destructive or anxious. Mental enrichment (scent work, training, puzzle toys) is not optional — it's healthcare.
Ontario resources: Canadian Dutch Shepherd Club, Working Dog Association of Canada, and local Schutzhund/IGP clubs in the GTA are excellent starting points for sport training.
